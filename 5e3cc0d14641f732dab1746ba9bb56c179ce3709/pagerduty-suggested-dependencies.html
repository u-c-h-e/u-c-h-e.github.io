Milestone 2
Problem: Responders unaware of undefined dependencies
IT environments are becoming increasingly complex, and responders are not always aware of what service relationships are tied to their own. As a result, they struggle to efficiently and independently resolve around 50% of incidents that arise when they are on call. This can draw out the incident investigation process and negatively impact the company’s bottom line.
PagerDuty identified two key milestones they wanted to be addressed. This case study covers the second; to learn about the first, click here.
view project
Solution: Suggested Dependencies
The team’s machine-learning algorithm surfacing can automatically identify and suggest service dependencies. This keeps teams updated on changes and helps to prevent blind spots during incident triage.
Adapting Success Metrics*
While the first milestone focused on offering valuable information about user-defined dependencies, this milestone centered on creating and highlighting automatically-identified ones. As such, metrics were modified slightly:
Top success metrics:
  • % Rate of adoption of dependencies among those who see suggestion dependencies
  • % Engagement with service dependency data displayed
  • % of accounts that add dependencies
Why these metrics matter: Less time resolving incidents means improved employee productivity and satisfaction, while higher engagement indicates that users are getting value from the feature. Additionally, accounts that add dependencies are 37% more likely to be satisfied with the product and therefore expand their account with PagerDuty.

Framing The Challenge
I kicked off brainstorming by translating the problem statement into a “How might we…” (HMW) question:
How might we surface relevant information about services to responders during wartime?
This was used to explore new ideas and solutions to a specific design challenge at hand. Since it was a part of existing work, I also considered how and where adding suggested dependencies on the incident detail would fit into the existing user flow.

A few key callouts on things that needed to be included in Milestone 2:
  • An indication of the dependent service being suggested and not defined
  • The ability to provide feedback/confirm dependency
  • Placement of suggested dependency in relation to user-defined ones
  • State of confirmed dependency and state of error on ‘Yes’

Exploring Potential Design Solutions
I explored ways to add suggested dependencies, as well as differentiate them from user-defined ones. The main challenge I encountered incorporating suggested dependencies into the design was communicating its difference in the most concise way possible. I didn’t want users to feel overwhelmed.

Early hi-fi mocks of suggested dependencies
The existing PagerDuty icon used to communicate machine-learning is a wand. Unfortunately, it wasn’t widely used or well understood. When showing my explorations to the team, they were stumped on what the icon meant, which made me curious to hear users' reactions to it.
“Is that icon a stick? It looks like a stick.”
Validating Designs with Target Users
I hand-picked five people who matched the target user persona; the group had limited years of software development and on-call experience and were new to the company. While testing my designs with them, I uncovered new challenges unique to this user group.
"I'm not sure what those red icons mean. Maybe that something's wrong with those services?"
Turns out, these users weren’t absorbing the new information. It took them a while to grasp what was upstream versus downstream, and they had a limited understanding of existing iconography (not just the ML wand). This was likely due to a combo of having limited experience with the overall product and the confusing order of information within the platform.
I realized this user group needed simpler, more digestible information. I sought out additional thoughts and feedback from my design peers before jumping into additional iterations. The feedback gathered at this stage helped birth an improved design.

Clearer copy to accompany icons
I used simple language that clearly communicated information, instead of relying on iconography that people could misinterpret.
Reordering information
I simplified the order of information. Prior to this, the placement of the service for the incident delayed user understanding of what they were looking for and appeared to be redundant information.
Total count of services
I added the service count for user-defined dependencies. Users wanted to know the blast radius of an issue, and the earlier design did not provide that information.
Back to the Validation Board (Round 2)
I followed up with the 5 testers I'd spoken to earlier and tested the new designs with them to see how well my changes tracked. All 5 preferred the later designs, citing that it was easier to absorb the data in the new layout.
"Oh, this is nice. You're giving me the total number of services. I like that extra context.”
Testing these iterations was vital in confirming that the changes addressed previous concerns and improved users’ understanding and overall experience.

Key Insights 💡
Icon-ing is hard
No matter how well designed, we can’t assume that users will understand what icons are for. When people have a hard time identifying what an icon is or what it means, it’s time to reevaluate.

Compromise is an inherent part of design
‍Due to time constraints, it wasn’t possible to launch with the new and improved designs. Instead, I ended up making compromises with product and engineering to ensure that the project launched on time. This entailed creating a hybrid design that incorporated some improvements, but not all.
Refined Key Features After Testing
Our timeline was tight because we were trying to release before PagerDuty’s annual summit. During the summit, we realized that accessibility was a challenge and implementation caused some wonky behavior on the site. Afterward, we swapped out the hybrid design for the latest designs. My accordion design resolved the unexpected behavior, though it required some additional work on the back end. Below is our post-summit release.

Lessons Learned
Kill your darlings. You might really believe in the vision of the designs (as you should!), but don't get too attached. It might not be possible to move forward with your design for any multitude of reasons. Always be ready to let go of designs as you iterate and keep moving forward.
Take the reins on research. The product owner and I heard from a diverse group of users during the earlier milestone, when it likely would've been more useful to talk to a more targeted group. Going into the second milestone, I led the research and was very selective and diligent about finding people who fit our target persona. This led to uncovering issues that directly impacted our target users and helped strengthen the final product.
