Milestone 1
Problem: There's no such thing as free lunch (forever)
Ribbon, a fintech startup in real estate, is known for helping homebuyers and their agents make all-cash offers for an added fee. At the time of this project, Appraisal Protection (AP) was an exclusive, complimentary service Ribbon provided homebuyers to strengthen their all-cash offers by removing appraisal contingencies. If a home under-appraised, Ribbon would cover the difference at no additional cost.
However, with the real estate markets being so hot in 2021 (and remaining extremely competitive today), homes would quickly sell for tens of thousands over list price. AP soon became an unsustainable offering that was costing Ribbon hundreds of thousands of dollars on a frequent basis. Unless Ribbon make some changes, ¬†AP would continue to generate significant losses for the company.
view project
Solution: Sustainable Appraisal Protection options
We ended creating a new, paid version of AP that gave homebuyers and stopped Ribbon from continuously being on the hook for the full appraisal gaps. I designed the screens in our app that allows agents to select the paid offering that would work best for their clients. Additionally, I fleshed out the product pages that showcased, but most importantly, explained the new offerings on our company website.
Researching preferences and acceptance criteria
We needed to make important updates to Appraisal Protection, but there wasn't a clear picture of a) the new version should look like and what buyers and b) their agents would be willing to accept. The consequences of launching a new product that was too complex for agents or buyers to understand was a major, valid concern, especially when it came to pricing and fees.

I stepped in when there were solely ideas floating around about what Appraisal Protection. The most popular ones were Pay Now and Pay Later.
view project
1. Pay Now
Homebuyers could pay a non-refundable fee upfront and get coverage in the event that there is a gap. (This option is very similar to traditional insurance.)
2. Pay Later
If there was an appraisal gap, homebuyers could defer payment of that gap until they sold or refinanced their home. Alternatively, they could bring cash to cover the gap at closing.
After attending meetings with stakeholders that included the CEO and Director of Finance, I created a research plan detailing the problem along with the objectives, methodology, and specific questions we would ask agents and homebuyers. When product management was added to the team, I worked closely with the product to further flesh out the interview and survey questions.

My approach for the first phase of research was to uncover what sort of financial product would have the highest likelihood of success if we were to launch it. There were two objectives:

Analyzing data to inform product and design decisions
We started off with a round of interviews with buyers and agents to begin learn how they would react to the "hypothetical" changes and what their expectations would be around cost. Product and I interviewed over 20 agents and homebuyers. Additionally, we drafted a survey that collected more feedback more quickly and was used to identify and recruit more homebuyers to interview.
Both the qualitative and quantitative research validated my hypothesis that despite the complexity around the cost and legal terms the Pay Later option posed, more homebuyers preferred that option. The two main callouts from the first phase of research were:

The math for the Pay Later option was tricky. On calls, buyers and agents would spend a good portion of the calls go through hypothetical offer amounts, appraisal gaps, and more to try to better understand how much each option might cost.
Design approach
Using the data collected from user research, it became apparent that prioritizing learnability was going to be absolutely essential for a product launch. If people struggled with how each financial offering worked, they would be less likely to use Ribbon to create an offer. I created product pages that would live on the company website to explain what the new Appraisal Protections options were. My main objectives were to:
  • Meet the audience where they were at. Homebuyers were the end-users so I was very intentional about simplifying content so that agents and buyers alike could understand the content on the public-facing product pages.
  • Let the content breath. As not not overwhelming people with paragraph and paragraphs of text, I thought about was to visually break down information and let people absorb the information.
I shared iterations of the designs internally with my team and stakeholders to collect feedback early before conducting testing and additional research with buyers and their agents.

Polishing up the product page designs
In the end, I ending up making iterations to the landing, pricing, and FAQs pages that I had conducted usability testing on. On the landing page, a few key updates I made we based on observations and feedback received during testing. The following were made for the landing page:
  1. People skip to pricing and miss out on valuable info: This led to removing the buttons higher up on the AP landing page.
  2. Agents and buyers liked having options: While homebuyers and agents gravitated more towards Pay Later, there were enough that preferred the alternative, Pay Now, which lead to us prioritizing launching with of both options.
  3. What happens in different scenarios was unclear: Agents and buyers weren't 100% what would happen in different scenarios based on what AP program they chose. I decided to create things down for them visually so they had a clear way of grasping what would happen in different scenarios.

Polishing up the app designs
While the product pages I designed focused more on education, the product screens with in the app focused more on functionality. I took a holistic view at the user flow, determine would be the best place to slot in a the ability to make a selection on AP within the product, and assessed how much educational content ought to live within the product versus outside.

The core element of the in-app design was the product card that I created to provide agents (who are the only ones who can create an offer in our product) with important details (such as cost). ¬†For the in-app AP screen, I focused on making it easy to compare options, access more information, and help their clients make a decision.
