overview

It can be challenging to keep files and folders organized. When it comes to managing content on the Cloud, it is easy to create and upload work without giving much thought to where it is best placed for future reference. Responding to this need, spaceKit is a conceptual Cloud storage application that provides users with the organizational support and flexibility they need to store content as they see fit.

Discovery

HOUSTON, WE HAVE A PROBLEM

Organizing files on the cloud (and off of it) can be a pain

While the problem I was working to solve was related to organization, I identified other user challenges that could improve Cloud storage apps generally. These issues emerged as part of the larger story about how managing or organizing shapes a user’s experiences with Cloud storage apps. Specifically, I prioritized and framed the core challenges as:

  1. It's difficult to remember to save content in the right location every time;
  2. Cluttered dashboards make content hard to find; and
  3. There isn't a way to save links in an easily accessible and organized manner.

ASSUMPTIONS

Setting eyes on the target

When it comes to files storage, most people fit into one of these groups:

  1. The Minimalist - uses Cloud storage to back-up essential files only.
  2. The Collector - uses Cloud storage for everything.
  3. The InBetweener - uses Cloud storage for a moderate amount of files.

My focus (and empathy) was with the In-Betweeners, as they generally have a wealth of content and sometimes find it difficult to keep track of everything. Knowing this, I wanted to create a versatile app that would help them organize their content in a streamlined and intuitive way.

SOLUTIONS

Marie Kondo meets Cloud storage

With a clear vision of the persona and the problem I was solving, I began brainstorming about the solutions a new Cloud storage app should offer. The top four items included:

  1. Ensuring that dashboards remain clutter-free;
  2. Encouraging users to organize their content in a helpful and unobtrusive manner;
  3. Establishing an easy-to-understand hierarchy for saving content; and
  4. Providing alternative organizational methods above and beyond folders.

Visual Design

BRANDING

The cloud app next door

Because spaceKit is meant to be a secure space for people to store their ideas, inspiration, and work (including works in progress), I wanted the brand to embody the themes of playfulness, creativity and adventure. I opted for spaceKit, a name that would instill more trust yet maintain a level of creativity and interest users could be drawn into.

view style guide

Logo

Rockets and spaceships are staples in logo design, but I decided to design one with a bit more flair. I did this by adding a slide/staircase leading to a spaceship. Even after I changed the brand name, the design still meshed well with the theme of space, creativity, and playfulness so much so that I decided to stick with it.

Typography

The font pair originally selected was Didact Gothic and PT Sans, but I later swapped the secondary font (PT Sans) to Lato. Testing

PROTOTYPES

Usability testing

I conducted three phases of usability tests. In the first batch, users were tasked with signing up for an account, adding a new piece of content to their dashboard, and organizing a file. The second batch was conducted after I created the high fidelity wireframes and required a new set of testers to create a folder, customize their folder, select the section displaying, and more.

Top bar: providing more focus

Multiple changes were made to the design based on feedback received at this stage. One trend than came up with most testers was that they had a hard time spotting the key button to sort items in their dashboard. The simple solution was to update the top bar by moving the upgrade button and changing the color of the sort button.

Folder images: more customization, better organization

Additional options for folder covers: I also received feedback that it would be helpful to have additional options to customize their folder covers. Originally, option A was to drag and drop an image file to the front of the folder they wanted to update. The alternative Option B was to right-click the bottom of the folder card and select ‘Edit folder image’ from the dropdown menu.

Before: Right-clicking to edit folder image

Auto-populated banner images & stock: For users without a particular image in mind to use for a folder cover, there needed to be a way to help them select a cover without it feeling like a pesky additional step. The solution was to have a modal appear after users create a folder where they could also chose:

  • an image from the folder they uploaded, or

  • a photo from one of spaceKit’s free stock, or

  • a color for their folder.

Modal providing users with options for editing folder covers

Key insights

Many users mentioned how much they liked the vibrant colors, the display controls, and how easy to navigate and familial the interface was. The editable folder cover was also a big hit! That said, more exploration needed to be done on what other features could be added to the MVP to make the user experience better.

Breadcrumbs to improve navigation

To make sure users have clear insight into exactly where files are located, I added breadcrumbs to the sticky top navigation bar. I played around with the idea of tabs where users could select between Folders, Files, and Links but realized that would be redundant if users could just drill down with folders.

Reflection continued

Lesson #1: Poor direction is the biggest mistake you could make

Okay, so that probably

got your attention.

The importance of having a clear idea and vision from the beginning of a large project cannot be overstated. When I started this project, I was biased in thinking that there wasn't much that could be improved in Cloud storage. Later in the process, I realized I was wrong. I eventually found the right direction, but lost valuable time in the process. I learned to focus more time on the research stage, in formulating and asking the right questions to ensure I was heading in the right direction.



Lesson #2: Surveys are helpful, but only when you have (proper) direction

Tied to lesson one, a symptom of having poor direction is not knowing the right questions to ask. If I could do this project over, I would have created a secondary survey to ask respondents about their specific behaviors and preferences when it comes to organizing content. For future projects, I will spend plenty of time trying to understand problems users face before I begin working out solutions.


Lesson #3: Have a key differentiator

My differentiator came to me at a later stage, after interacting with usability testers and having one-on-one conversations with them. I learned that pretty design is not enough to engage users and create value for them. If there isn't something unique about your product that none of your competitors already have, it is necessary to go back to square one.
