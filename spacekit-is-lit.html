Problem: Cloud storage services offer extremely limited features around content organization.
With as easy as it is to store things in the cloud, it’s no wonder that a user’s collection can quickly become unwieldy—but it’s also true that the cloud storage market is expected to only continue to grow, reaching $106.71 billion by 2024.
Providers have not yet risen to the challenge of helping their users stay organized and create sustainable ways of maintaining their content, which can prove frustrating for users. As someone who uses cloud storage to back up many valuable files, I know this struggle firsthand.
view project
Solution: Marie Kondo x Cloud Storage
spaceKit wants to ease those user frustrations, as a web and mobile application catering to visual creatives (think: designers, videographers, video editors, photographers, etc.) who need a bit of help organizing their work. It does so by:
  • Moving users toward saving content in designated spaces 
  • Providing a de-cluttered dashboard and overall storage space
  • Making it easier for users to find content
Identifying the People Problem
Before design can truly begin, we must know who the product is for. While a general target market may seem attractive, if your target market is everyone, your target market is actually no one. The initial creative brief I received from spaceKit was very broad, so I approached the research phase as an opportunity to get specific about the subset of cloud storage users whose needs were not being fully met.

This gave me a better understanding of the gaps a new product in the space could fill.

Surveys
I chose to create a survey in order to quickly capture a representative sample of the larger population I was targeting. The survey was a blend of attitudinal questions to get a snapshot of the current experiences and perspectives people have around cloud storage. I included questions on app usage, most used features, and pain points or frustrations.
Turned out, a good chunk of the 50+ survey responses validated my sneaking suspicion that organization was a pain point for cloud storage users. I noticed an interesting trend in the data: people with more creative backgrounds such as photography, design, etc. voiced the most challenges around organization. Based on what I saw from the preliminary survey results, I wanted to dive deeper and uncover the why behind this issue through user interviews.

User Interviews
I scheduled half-hour chats with a handful of survey respondents to talk more about their backgrounds, challenges, and frustrations. One key insight that cropped up repeatedly among my discussions revealed that users feel existing cloud storage providers make it too easy for them to clutter their dashboards with disorganized content. Many people—from a hobbyist photographer, to a freelance designer, to an entrepreneur—echoed this sentiment.

Key Insights 💡
The dark side of drag & drop.
While probing into why cluttered dashboards were so ubiquitous, I realized that dragging and dropping content in the cloud—while a user-friendly interface—makes it all too easy to store things and forget about them. Organizing becomes an afterthought and at a certain point, it’s too overwhelming to even start that process.
Actions speak louder than words (sometimes).
Even as some users said that organization features were not a high priority, they also shared common pain points around how difficult it was to find their stored content. A key takeaway for me here was that when surveys yield conflicting results, it’s up to me as the UX expert and designer to intuitively analyze what I see and make sense of it. 
Target Users
After processing and sifting through my user research and responses, there were obvious similarities among potential customers. By giving real-life stories, faces, and names to these target users, I could refer to them and ask myself questions like, What would Quandra think of this design decision? How would this addition serve Jessie’s needs? Asking questions like this, and holding my target users in my mind, was key to reaching the MLP (Minimum Lovable Product).

Prioritizing Features by Defining Users’ Goals
After surveys, interviews, and competitive analyses were complete, it was time to shift my focus to establish the MVP priorities for the functionality the cloud app should provide. I find job stories are an effective tool to understand the user and the problem they need solved, and I chose to use them here. I wanted to be sure I was designing for the problem first, instead of the solution.
I then converted the job stories into user flows to illustrate the process and number of steps required for users to achieve a meaningful goal.

Design Direction Validation
There’s nothing like putting your work out there in the wild for users to engage with. I prioritize getting feedback early and often, since I tend to make product decisions based on information collected from users.
It was important to confirm whether or not concepts at this stage were clear and the foundational designs and page elements were in line with the relevant goals and content. I used Maze to test mission-based tasks and InVision to conduct in-person usability tests using the low-fidelity wireframe clickable prototype. Both served as key tools that helped me learn where I needed to revise.

Iterating Based on Feedback
The Maze and Invision tests both served as key tools that helped me learn where I needed to revise my designs. I made changes to both the UI and overall UX experiences based on the feedback received from testers. I've highlighted a few key changes below

Make tagging feature more prominent
Most users were uncertain of how to go about adding tags to content. To resolve this, I pulled the tagging option from the right-click menu and added a tag/label icon users could click to add a tag.
Simplify top bar to add visibility to key features
Users had a hard time spotting the button to sort items due to its being in a “busy” section of the dashboard. To reduce visual clutter, I shifted the Upgrade button to the sidebar and removed the info icon. Prototype Validation
After addressing usability challenges and polishing the app's aesthetics, I went through a final phase of testing to see how visual and usability changes tracked with users. I gave a new set of testers a more robust set of tasks, including ones around the key takeaways I discovered. Here were the results:
  • All eight users were able to articulate the purpose of "Categories".
  • 80% of testers were able to find the option to tag a file in 38% less time.
  • The time it took testers to find the sort button went down 65%.
The changes I made drastically reduced the time it took for users to complete tasks, and feedback around the design was positive. Users commented on how clean and sleek the interface felt. The biggest callouts were around the customizable folder covers—which was one of the few MLP (Minimum Lovable Product) features I kept in the design. Users also loved the new tag feature.

Key Insights 💡
Testing unconventional moves requires even more feedback than usual.
Because the product introduced new concepts, I employed moderated usability testing as much as possible. Hearing testers think out loud as they worked through their opinions and reactions to the interface was invaluable; it also gave me the chance to ask follow-up questions and gauge their understanding in real time. 
Refined Key Features After Testing
By focusing primarily on user needs, I was able to drive design decisions forward and ultimately design a user experience that addresses the organizational shortcomings of existing cloud storage services. The clouds parted and spaceKit emerged, a web-app experience allowing users to take control of their organization. Buh-bye, cluttered dashboards! Au revoir, hard to find files! Hello, clean dashboard and organized content!

Lessons Learned
The earlier you find a hypothesis to test, the better. There's value in keeping ideas and problem spaces broad and general at the start of a project to uncover all possibilities. However, the benefit of having a specific, focused hypothesis early in the exploratory phase makes it possible to draft highly relevant, targeted questions to ask folks and increases the chance you’ll get more useful, applicable answers.
Balance feedback with time constraints. Throughout the process, I learned a lot about what people expect from their task management apps. I received various suggestions during user research and testing for new features and ways to take the app to the next level. Naturally, I wanted to implement all of them; however, I quickly learned how to soak in feedback and prioritize my energy and focus to work within time constraints.

If I Had More Time...
Given more time, I would explore a few other key features :
  • Suggested tags would make it easier for users in a rush to add a bit of organization to their content and later find their files and links.
  • Advanced search capabilities, such as suggested searches, would make it easier for users to search content
  • The ability to add additional tags from the dashboard would save users a few clicks and make the overall process more efficient
