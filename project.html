Overview

Keeping track of all of our files and the various places we’ve put them is often easier said than done. When it comes to managing our content on the cloud, it’s easy to create or upload our work without giving much further thought into where we’ve actually placed them.


As we continue to grow increasingly reliant on cloud storage, it’s important to find a solution that allows us to do more than store our work. spaceKit is a cloud storage application that provides users organizational support and the flexibility to customize their content as they see fit.

Discovery

HOUSTON, WE HAVE A PROBLEM

Organizing files on the cloud (and off of it) can be a pain

While organization was the problem using I was solving, there were other user challenges that fit into the scope of improvements cloud storage apps could benefit from. They were all a part of the larger story of how managing or organizing shapes users’ experiences with cloud storage apps. I prioritized all of the core challenges as:

  1. It's difficult to remember to save content to the right location every time.
  2. Cluttered dashboards make it hard to find content.
  3. There isn't a way to save links in an easy-accessible and organized manner.
SOLUTIONS

Marie Kondo meets cloud storage

With a better vision of the type of user I wanted to “solve” for, I begun brainstorming the solutions a new cloud storage app should address:

Key insights

The cloud storage market is quite saturated, but researching top contenders in the space such as Google Drive, Dropbox, and Mega provided me with valuable insight into where a new competitor could fit into the mix. In order to stand any chance of carving out a meaningful piece of the market, a new cloud storage provider would have to:

  • provide more storage space for free users

  • end-to-end data encryption

  • offer some sort of competitive advantage when it comes to content organization

USER RESEARCH

Swiping left to google drive

So what do users want the most from cloud storage? To answer that question, I shifted my focus from making assumptions about my target users to hearing directly from them. I drafted a survey to find out the type of features cloud storage users value the most. It touched slightly on organization but the focus was to learn generally behavior and motivations for a typical user. Feedback from the 50 cloud users that participated in my survey helped me answer:

  1. What do users primarily use cloud storage for?
  2. What are their motivations for using the cloud storage apps that they use?
  3. What type of content do they store on the cloud?
  4. What features would they like to see?
  5. What are their main frustrations?

Visual Design

BRANDING

The cloud app next door

Because spaceKit is meant to be secure space for people to store their ideas, inspiration, work (and works in progress), I wanted the brand to embody the themes of playfulness, creativity and adventure. The platform was originally to be call ‘playground’, but that was later changed because I didn’t believe that brand name would translate well for business users. I opted for spaceKit, a name that would instill more trust yet maintain a level of creativity and interest users could be drawn into.

PROTOTYPES

Usability testing

I conducted two phases of usability tests. For the first batch, users were tasked with signing up for an account, adding a new piece of content to their dashboard, and organizing a file. The second batch was conducted after I created the high fidelity wireframes and required a new set of testers to create a folder, customize their folder, select the section displaying, and more..

Top bar: providing more focus

Multiple changes were made to the design based on feedback received in this stage. One trend than came up with most testers was that they had a hard time spotting the button to sort items in their dashboard. It was in a busy section of the navigation, the top right corner.

Before: upgrade button drew users into the CTA

After: sort button drew users into a key function

Folder images: more customization, better organization

Additional options for folder covers: I also received feedback that it would be helpful to have additional options to customize their folder covers. Originally there were only two ways achieve that. Option A was to drag and drop an image file to the front of the folder they wanted to update. The alternative was to right-click the bottom of the folder card and select ‘Edit folder image’ from the dropdown menu.

After: sort button drew users into a key function

Auto-populated banner images & stock: For users without a particular image they wanted to use in mind for a folder cover, there needed to be a way to help them select an cover without it feeling like a pesky additional step. The solution was to have a modal appear after users create a folder where they could also:

  • chose an image from the folder they uploaded, or

  • could choose a picture from one of spaceKit’s free stock.

Adding color as an option: I took for updates for the folders section on step further based on more user feedback. Users wanted the option to just change the color of a folder cover versus selecting an image. Some users like to organize their physical files and notes by color, therefore, in order to allow them transfer that same behavior to their online workspace as well, this alternative was added.


Clarifying upgrade CTA: Additionally, users felt that the upgrade button in the dropdown menu that appeared when they clicked on the user avatar did not provide enough context. They were uncertain about what the word upgrade was in reference to. To clear up the confusion, I updated the dropdown menu to clarify upgrading was in reference to storage space.

Final Product

THE SELF-INFLICTED DESIGN CRITIQUE

Finding the missing ingredients

While the user testers had managed to complete the majority of tasks and liked the overall design, it still felt as though there were a few things missing from the prototype. I decided to take it a step further to focus more the organization goal of the product. I hadn’t quite address the challenge of making or. I felt that while the users like the design, I could take it a step further and polish a few things such as shadows and typography to make the whole thing pop.

Breadcrumbs to improve navigation

To make sure users have clear insight into exactly where files are located, I added breadcrumbs to the sticky top navigation bar. I played around with the idea of tabs where users could select between Folders, Files and Links but realized that would be redundant if users could just drill down with folders.

Before: Provided the option to choose only images as folder covers

Tags for even more organization

I looked back at my original survey results and noted how 20% of users tags/labels to organize their files in general. I wondered if implenting the ability to add tags would help users sort through related content that may not necessarily like in the same primary folder.

I looked back at my original survey results and noted how 20% of users tags/labels to organize their files in general. I wondered if implenting the ability to add tags would help users sort through related content that may not necessarily like in the same primary folder.

Stronger hierarchy for a cleaner dashboard

Adding another layer on top of folders and files (and links) could help avoid a cluttered with files that were uploaded without being stored in any specific location dashboard and encourage better organizatied content. Users would also be able to keep their work vs personal content better separted this way if they only have on spaceKit account.

Save to the right place from jump

Users mentioned wanting their cloud storage app to take more of the reins in terms of organization. While adding a tab to control the display that did not actually tackle to core issue of making sure files are stored where they’re supposed to be. In order to make that a part of the user’s flow with the app,

Unlike Google drive and Dropbox that allow users to upload files the the main dashboard where they can become unorganize and lost, I added a step to the flow for creating or upload files. If the user if on the home dashboard and drag and drop a file or try to upload any piece of content, spaceKit will probe them about where they want the folder, file or link to be saved to.


By incorcorpating that step, users save content to the right location from the start vs having to go back to their home dashboard in the future and then move files over to the correct location.

More reflection in the mix

Lesson 1: Listen to feedback early on and try to come up with a resolution then. When it came to the navigation bar, I didn’t make changes to it’s overall design early on. That was a silly decision on my part because the next round of usability tests, users continued to struggle to find the button.


Lesson 2: It was then that I realized something had the changed, and so I went back to the drawing board and came up with a solution. I tested said solution out of my final user, and oh boy was the experience night and day! Now I know that the more testing you do, the better the product you design will be and it’s alright to love an idea, but you have to be will to part with it if it just ain’t working!
