overview

Keeping track of all of our files and the various places we’ve put them is often easier said than done.


When it comes to managing our content on the cloud, it’s easy to create or upload our work without giving much further thought into where we’ve actually placed them. spaceKit is a conceptual cloud storage application that provides users organizational support and the flexibility to customize their content as they see fit.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

Know thy enemy, know thyself

To gain insight into where spaceKit would sit in the market, I had to conduct research on what was tools users already had at their disposal. I focused my attention on companies who dominate the cloud storage and organization space.

  • Lack of organizing features

Key insights

The cloud storage market is quite saturated, but researching top contenders in the space such as Google Drive, Dropbox, and Mega provided me with valuable insight into where a new competitor could fit into the mix. In order to stand any chance of carving out a meaningful piece of the market, a new cloud storage provider would have to:

  • provide more storage space for free users

  • end-to-end data encryption

  • offer some sort of competitive advantage when it comes to content organization

BRANDING

The cloud app next door

Because spaceKit is meant to be secure space for people to store their ideas, inspiration, work (and works in progress), I wanted the brand to embody the themes of playfulness, creativity and adventure. I opted for spaceKit, a name that would instill more trust yet maintain a level of creativity and interest users could be drawn into.

Testing

WIREFRAMES & PROTOTYPES

The devil in the details

After incorporating the highest priority user flows into my initial wireframes, I began testing to gather feedback early in my design flow. The feedback received revolved around provide more flexibility around the dashboard display, and making the top-right section of the navigation bar less busy.

PROTOTYPES

Usability testing

I conducted three phases of usability tests. In the first batch, users were tasked with signing up for an account, adding a new piece of content to their dashboard, and organizing a file. The second batch was conducted after I created the high fidelity wireframes and required a new set of testers to create a folder, customize their folder, select the section displaying, and more...

Top bar: providing more focus

Multiple changes were made to the design based on feedback received in this stage. One trend than came up with most testers was that they had a hard time spotting the button to sort items in their dashboard. It was in a busy section of the navigation, the top right corner.

Before: upgrade button drew users into the CTA

After: sort button drew users into a key function

Folder images: more customization, better organization

Additional options for folder covers: I also received feedback that it would be helpful to have additional options to customize their folder covers. Originally option A was to drag and drop an image file to the front of the folder they wanted to update. The alternative was to right-click the bottom of the folder card and select ‘Edit folder image’ from the dropdown menu.

After: sort button drew users into a key function

Auto-populated banner images & stock: For users without a particular image they wanted to use in mind for a folder cover, there needed to be a way to help them select an cover without it feeling like a pesky additional step. The solution was to have a modal appear after users create a folder where they could also:

  • chose an image from the folder they uploaded, or

  • could choose a picture from one of spaceKit’s free stock.

Before: Option for stock images added

After: Option for color and stock images

Adding color as an option: Some users like to organize their physical files and notes by color, therefore, in order to allow them transfer that same behavior to their online workspace, this simple feature was added.


Clarifying upgrade CTA: Additionally, users felt that the upgrade button in the dropdown menu was confusing. Because they were uncertain about what the word upgrade was in reference to, I updated the dropdown menu to clarify upgrading was in reference to storage space.

Key insights

Biggest takeaway? Sweat the details. I was 'eh' about my design even though the reactions to it was positive from the poll of designers, developers and x I had test it. But I thought it could be taken a step further so I went back and polished my work.

Final Product

THE SELF-INFLICTED DESIGN CRITIQUE

Finding the missing ingredients

While the user testers had managed to complete the majority of tasks and liked the overall design, it still felt as though there were a few things missing from the prototype.while the users, it didn't have the organizational emphasis it needed to. So I decided to fix that.

Breadcrumbs to improve navigation

To make sure users have clear insight into exactly where files are located, I added breadcrumbs to the sticky top navigation bar. I played around with the idea of tabs where users could select between Folders, Files and Links but realized that would be redundant if users could just drill down with folders.

Before: Provided the option to choose only images as folder covers

Before: Provided the option to choose only images as folder covers

Tags for even more organization

I looked back at my original survey results and noted how 20% of users tags/labels to organize their files in general. I wondered if implenting the ability to add tags would help users sort through related content that may not necessarily like in the same primary folder.

I looked back at my original survey results and noted how 20% of users tags/labels to organize their files in general. I wondered if implenting the ability to add tags would help users sort through related content that may not necessarily like in the same primary folder.

Stronger hierarchy for a cleaner dashboard

Adding another layer on top of folders and files (and links) could help avoid a cluttered with files that were uploaded without being stored in any specific location dashboard and encourage better organizatied content. Users would also be able to keep their work vs personal content better separted this way if they only have on spaceKit account.

Save to the right place from go

Users mentioned wanting their cloud storage app to take more of the reins in terms of organization. While adding a tab to control the display that did not actually tackle to core issue of making sure files are stored where they’re supposed to be. In order to make that a part of the user’s flow with the app,

Unlike Google drive and Dropbox that allow users to upload files the the main dashboard where they can become unorganize and lost, I added a step to the flow for creating or upload files.


spaceKit probes users about where they want the folder, file or link to be saved to if they try to drag and drop a file or try to upload any piece of content while on the main dashboard. By incorcorpating that step, users would save make their make their own lives easier by content to the right location from the start.

Reflection continued

Lesson #1: Poor direction is the devil.

Okay, so that

got your attention.

But it can't be understate the importance of having a clear idea. I started this project without a clear vision of what I was solving for. It look took a user asking me..si what are you solving for and me being like...uh?It looks better...? If you're not solving for a problem, it's important to rethink why you're design something.



Lesson #2: Feature creep is real.
Listen to feedback early on and try to come up with a resolution then. When it came to the navigation bar, I didn’t make changes to it’s overall design early on. That was a silly decision on my part because the next round of usability tests.


Lesson #3: Your first idea is not your best idea.
Iterations are inevitable. Step your game up if you can make got the time:Now I know that the more testing you do, the better the product you design will be. It’s alright to love an idea, but you have to be willing to part with it if it just ain’t working!


Lesson #4: Have a key differentiator
This one is a a bit like entrepreunership 101. Ties in strongly with lesson 2. My differentiator came to me in the testing stage which x. But I plunged forward since someyimes you don't have the time to coe up with a grand idea and if you do, you'll next start.
